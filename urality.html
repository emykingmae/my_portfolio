The Consumer Experience of Urality

Uniting and empowering local businesses by connecting people to their communities through discovery.

We’re flipping the internet paradigm of "search first" to "location first."
- Justin Copenhaver, CEO

Overview and project conception

Everything Urality does aims to support communities through conecting local business, community leaders, and creators with people physically in their community (whether local or vistor). The Customer Experience (defined as CX herewith) for Urality seeks to provide an answer to the question, “What’s going on in this community?”

The goal for this project was to create a CX in the form of a web application that connects users through location/proximity, with a heavy emphasis on discovery of “what’s nearby.” By doing this, users of Urality would be able explore through the CX the following four things:


  1. 1) Local events
  2. 2) Local businesses
  3. 3) Have a “Discovery” experience
  4. 4) View nearby posts (Urality/Twitter/Instagram)

Defining the Problem

In a digital world of SEO and direct ads, how can local businesses and community leaders reach the people in their communities directly? And vice versa, how can locals connect directly with what is happening in their communities through on-location discovery and organic experiences?

The problem was best defined as a question: How can we best connect local businesses and community leaders/creators with people in the community (and vice versa)?

The target destination was defined as towns with 10k - 75k local residents.

Identifying a Solution

The design brings users in direct contact with what is happening near them through an experience of discovery. Four main tabs provide this insight:

  1. The Nearby Tab - Features local posts from Creators, Businesses, and other local entities (and also pulls in curated relevant content from Twitter and Instagram)

  2. The Events Tab - Delivers upcoming filterable events based on user location/proximity

  3. The Business Tab - Connects users directly with businesses by location/proximity, with filterable options

  4. The Discover Tab - An interactive experience wherein the user narrows down results based on filtering down through categories (top level categories being Food, Drink, and Fun)

Consulting the research and positioning the project

The guiding research for this project (and a central mantra of Urality) indicated that location-based content was our priority.

Users can find anything they are looking for using services under the Google umbrella, or products like Yelp. The missing link for users (and for communities) is the connection and open dialogue between what’s local, who is being discovered, and how users can find them. SEO and paid ad spots often block an organic connection between people in a community and local business. Our CX would aim to better connect communities through proximity and discovery.

Sketching

I began sketching in desktop view, since this would be the view with the most content on display at once. I conducted four rounds of sketching out each tab, with team feedback and discussion between each one. Each iteration we made adjustments. We saw the biggest changes with the Discover tab, which really transformed through three stages of sketching.

Nearby

The idea for the Nearby tab was that users would see posts native to Urality, or curated Instagram/Twitter posts that were “reposted” within the app by a Urality profile. The goal of this initial sketching was to produce lo-fi wireframes of layout and essential components.

Discover Tab

We began to ideate more on what the Discover tab was meant to accomplish, and if we were serving the user. By the third round, we sketched a fresh idea for the tab that would provide a filter-down experience of categories, which became the solution we went with. By breaking discovery into three cats (Food, Drink, and Fun), we could help organize generally what the user was looking for, and provide an interactive “discovery” experience.

Business Tab

The business tab originally featured an embedded map view (a feature already built out with functionality in another place within the Urality web app).

As we ideated through rounds, it was decided to abandon the map view and keep the business tab consistent with the Events tab layout. We also validated in our research that users were primarily focused on finding businesses based on category. By providing filtered results in order of proximity to the user, we were already serving that need for proximity (hence there was no need to display results on a map view). Next, we added a link/CTA within the pop-up view directly to Google Maps, in the case of users wanting to find a business on the map or seek directions.

Events

The layout for the Events tab stayed mainly untouched, aside from adjustments to the details on each card (to clear up user confusion in testing) and the filters used to narrow results. This layout is what we would carry over to the Business tab to maintain consistency.

Mid-Project Discovery

Throughout the project I kept a small group of core users from my research phase that I kept reaching out to with further questions and updates. One big realization that came to light at this point in the project was that users who run with an Apple Watch (or other wearable) would expect the ability to sync their runs into this app.

While the advance metrics and health monitoring stats that come from wearables might not be something we’d offer in a free version of this app, they would be important to consider when further designing out additional features for the premium subscribers (down the road).

At the very least, it would be critical for me to design this “sync” moment for users looking to connect their wearables to the app.

High-Fidelity Prototyping

As my hi-fi screens began to take form, I continued to resort back to the information I had gathered from user research. My task was to identify the visual solutions that focused around core features of the user experience: Miles run, average pace, total time, and map tracking.

It was also important to build an easy way for users to quickly modify essential settings to their run: miles vs. km, disabling auto-lock, and connecting a wearable GPS device.

Central to my project was the idea of users being able to use the app for one-off runs, without creating an account, and without saving data from their run. The onboarding screens I designed kept this idea front and center.

Conclusion and What I Learned

This was a quick project that I had limited resources with. If I had more time and a budget, I would want to conduct some rounds of user testing with a prototype of my high-fidelity screens. It would be ideal to work with a developer to build a basic version of this app in order to allow testing on a run.

I really struggled with balancing how to keep a running app profitable, and designing an app that doesn’t require sign-ups from every user. I do think it would be possible to allow users to use the app without creating an account, but I would want to test this theory on real users.
